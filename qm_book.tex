\chapter{The Simple Harmonic Oscillator}

When Energy is quantized, the only allowed values for
E = $\epsilon \hbar \omega$, are values that yield solutions in the physical Hilbert space: $E_n$ = $(n + \frac{1}{2}) \hbar \omega$, $(n) = 0, 1, 2,...$.  \\
For each value of (n), the corresponding polynomials is of ($n_{th}$) order, called Hermite polynomials. We know that: $\boxed{[x, p] = i \hbar}$, and that the Hamiltonian $\hat H$ = $\frac{p^2}{2m}$ $\frac{1}{2}$ $m$ $\omega^2 \hat x^2$.The Quantum Harmonic Oscillator is an extension of the Classical Oscillator. We expect that Energy eigenstates, only exist for certain quantized values of (E). This is a differential equation:
\begin{equation}
- \frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2 \psi (x)}{d x^2} + \frac{1}{2} m \omega^2 x^2 \psi (x) = E \psi (x)
\end{equation}

The length scale $L_0$ = $\sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{m \omega}}$, to be called the $\mathbf{\textbf{oscillator size\index{oscillator size}}}$, is as relevant to the harmonic oscillator as the Bohr radius ($a_0$), is to the Hydrogen atom. The ground state energy of the quantum oscillator, is ($\frac{\hbar \omega}{2}$), and ($L_0$), is precisely the Classical oscillation amplitude of an oscillator with that Energy. A recursion relation of ($\epsilon$ = $2n + 1$) must terminate, for us to get an Energy eigenstate. The corresponding physical Energies $E_n$ = $\frac{\hbar \omega}{2}$ $(2n + 1)$ = $\boxed{\hbar \omega$ $(n + \frac{1}{2})$, $n = 0,1,2,...}$.The spectrum of this result is non-degenerate, and all energy eigenstates of the harmonic oscillator are bound states of a one-dimensional potential. The polynomial solutions are normalized Hermite polynomials and satisfy $2^{nd}$ order differential equation. We can write the eigenstate as a function of the physical coordinate (x), from Hermite polynomials. \emph{The Energy eigenstates are real functions of (x)}. We have full orthonormality of the Energy eigenstates $\langle \varphi_n, \varphi \rangle$ = $\delta_{mn}$, from the normalization
constant ($N_n$), determined from the condition $\langle \varphi_n, \varphi_n \rangle$ = $\int_{- \infty}^\infty$ dx $\varphi_n$ (x) = $1$. ($\hat x)$  and ($\hat p$) are Hermitian operators. All Energy eigenstates satisfy Energy (E),  so E $\geq$ $\frac{1}{2}$ $\hbar \omega$, since $\langle \hat H \rangle_\psi$ = $E$ $\hat p$, is $\frac{\hbar}{i}$ $\frac{\partial}{\partial x}$ in \emph{Position} space, and $\hat x$ is $i \hbar$ $\frac{\partial}{\partial p}$ in \emph{Momentum} space. The normalized ($n_th$) excited state of the simple harmonic oscillator is:
\begin{equation}
\varphi_n \equiv \frac{1}{\sqrt N!} \underbrace{\hat a^{+}...\hat a^{+}}_\text{n} \psi_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt N!} (\hat a^{+})^n \psi_0
\end{equation}

\section{Rigid-Body Rotation}

Any general displacement of a rigid body can be represented by a translation plus a rotation. We split the problem of rigid body motion into two separate phases, one concerned solely with the translational motion of the body, the other, with its' rotational motion. The total
angular momentum divides naturally into contributions from the translation of the center of mass and, from the rotation about the center of mass.

The total angular momentum will involve the Cartesian coordinates of the center of mass, and
the Euler angle coordinates. The Potential Energy in a uniform gravitational field will depend only upon the Cartesian vertical coordinate of the center of gravity. The two groups of coordinates will then be completely separated, and the translational and rotational problems can be solved independently of each other. We will link derivatives relative to a coordinate system fixed at some point in the rigid body.

The derivatives relative to any rigid body axes must vanish, and with $\vec{\omega}$ as being the angular velocity vector appropriate to the first coordinate system.The angular momentum vector is related to the angular velocity by a linear transformation. The operator (I), a Tensor of the second rank, acting upon the vector $\vec{\omega}$, results in the physically new vector (L). This rotation can be expressed in terms of the Euler angles ($\phi$), ($\theta$), and ($\psi$). A proper choice of these angles will transform (I) into its diagonal form:
$(I_D)$ = $\begin{pmatrix}\mathit{I_1} & 0 & 0 \cr 0 & \mathit{I_2} & 0 \cr 0 & 0 & \mathit{I_3} \end{pmatrix}$, where $(I_1)$, $(I_2)$, and $(I_3)$, are the eigenvalues of (I), referred to as the components of the principal moment of inertia tensor. Three roots are the desired principal moments.
All directions perpendicular to the axis of symmetry are then alike, which is the mark of a double root to the equation. The principal axes are then the symmetry axis, and any two perpendicular axes in the plane normal to the symmetry axis. The principal moments of inertia cannot be negative, because as the diagonal elements in the principal axes system have the form of sums of squares. For one of the principal moments to vanish, all points of the body must be such that two coordinates of each particle are zero. See Figure~\ref{fig:Spinning top.pdf}  \\

Any two axes perpendicular to the line of the body will then be the other principal axes. Indeed, this is a limiting case of a body with an axis of symmetry passing through the origin. If two of the roots of the equation are equal, the inertia ellipsoid thus has two equal axes, and is an ellipsoid of revolution. If all three principal moments are equal, the inertia ellipsoid is a sphere.  \\
